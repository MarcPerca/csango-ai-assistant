Base inicial de conocimiento:

Los Csango o Csángó son una comunidad etnica y cultural asociada historicamente a poblaciones catolicas de Moldavia, en Rumania. Muchas fuentes los describen como un grupo relacionado con los hungaros o con hablantes de un dialecto hungaro arcaico, aunque su identidad y origen pueden ser interpretados de formas distintas segun la fuente y segun la propia persona o comunidad.

Cuando alguien pregunte donde viven, responde con prudencia:

- Principalmente en la region historica de Moldavia, en el este de Rumania.
- Especialmente en zonas de los condados de Bacau, Neamt, Iasi y alrededores.
- Tambien hay comunidades o grupos asociados en Transilvania, por ejemplo alrededor del paso de Ghimes-Palanca y en la zona de los Siete Pueblos cerca de Brasov.
- Puede haber diaspora o personas Csango viviendo en otros paises, pero el nucleo historico se asocia sobre todo con Moldavia rumana.

Lengua y cultura:

- Algunas comunidades han hablado variedades conocidas como dialecto csango, relacionado con el hungaro.
- Muchas personas Csango tambien hablan rumano, y el grado de uso del dialecto varia mucho entre familias y pueblos.
- La religion catolica romana ha sido un elemento importante en la identidad de muchas comunidades Csango.
- La musica, la danza, la memoria oral y las tradiciones locales son temas relevantes para documentar.

Csángós

The Csángós ([Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_language): Csángók; [Romanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_language): Ceangăi) are [ethnic Hungarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians) living mostly in the [Romanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romania) region of [Moldavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Moldavia), especially in [Bacău County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bac%C4%83u_County). The region where the Csángós live in Moldavia is known as [Csángó Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3_Land). Their traditional language, Csángó, a [Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_language) dialect, is currently used by only a minority of the Csángó population group.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-adatbank.transindex.ro-6) The majority belong to the [Roman Catholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_Romania) faith, influenced by [Orthodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy) [rites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rite_(Christianity)).

Some Csángós also live in [Transylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transylvania) (around the [Ghimeș-Palanca Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghime%C8%99-Palanca_Pass) and in the so-called [Seven Villages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Villages))[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-9) and in the village of [Oituz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lumina,_Constan%C8%9Ba) in [Northern Dobruja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Dobruja).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-10)

Etymology

It has been suggested that the name Csángó is the present participle of a Hungarian verb csángál meaning 'wander, as if going away'; purportedly by [sibilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sibilation), in the pronunciation of some Hungarian consonants by Csángó people.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-13)

Alternative explanations include the Hungarian word elcsángált, meaning 'wandered away', or the phrase csángatta a harangot 'ring the bell'.[[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]

The Finnish researcher [Yrjö Wichmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yrj%C3%B6_Wichmann) believed that probably the name of ceangău (csángó) did not come from a certain Hungarian tribe, but they were called those Transylvanian Szeklers who moved away from their comrades and settled in areas inhabited by Romanians, where they were, both materially and ideologically influenced by them and even Romanianized to a certain level.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-14) Ion Podea in the "Monograph of Brașov County" of 1938 mentioned that the ethnonym derives from the verb csángodni or ecsángodni and means 'to leave someone or something, to alienate someone or something that has left you'. This was used by the Szeklers in the case of other Romanianized Szeklers from the Ciuc area.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-15)

In some Hungarian dialects (the one from [Transylvanian Plain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transylvanian_Plain) and the Upper Tisza) csángó, cángó means 'wanderer'.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-16) In connection with this etymological interpretation, the linguist [Szilágyi N. Sándor](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Szil%C3%A1gyi_N._S%C3%A1ndor&action=edit&redlink=1) [[hu](https://hu.wikipedia.org/wiki/Szil%C3%A1gyi_N._S%C3%A1ndor)] made an analogy between the verb "to wander" with the ethnonyms "[kabars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabars)" and "[khazars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khazars)", which means the same thing.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-17)

According to the "Dictionary of the Hungarian Language", 1862; The etymological dictionary of the Hungarian language , [Budapest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budapest) 1967; The historical dictionary of the Hungarian lexicon from [Transylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transylvania) , [Bucharest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bucharest), 1978; The Explanatory Dictionary of the Hungarian Language , Hungarian Academy Publishing House, Budapest, 1972; The new dictionary of regionalisms , Hungarian Academy Publishing House, Budapest, 1979, the terms csangó, csángó are translated in 'walker', 'a person who changes his place'.

The historian [Nicolae Iorga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolae_Iorga) stated that the term comes from șalgăi (șálgó,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-:iorga-18) with the variants derived from the Hungarian sóvágó meaning 'salt cutter'[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-19)), name given to the [Szekler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Szekler) workers at [Târgu Ocna mine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C3%A2rgu_Ocna_mine).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-:iorga-18)

A theory of the historian Antal Horger relates that the ceangău comes from czammog, which refers to a shepherd who walks with the bludgeon after the herds. Another hypothesis of [Bernát Munkácsi](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bern%C3%A1t_Munk%C3%A1csi&action=edit&redlink=1) explains that the term comes from the verb csángani which in [Ciuc County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ciuc_County) means 'to mix'.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-:pal-20)

Origins

Migration of the Hungarian Csángós

The Hungarian and the international literature in this subject unanimously agree that the Csángós are of [Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians) origin, with varying assimilated elements of Romanian, German, Polish and Italian.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-21)

The Hungarians originated on the [Asian Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Steppe), as a tribal federation composed of [Ugric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ugric_languages) and [Turkic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_peoples) peoples.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-25)

The Csángós had historically been a rural and agricultural people, raising stock like sheep and cows and farmed crops such as corn, potatoes, and hemp. They were also often called for military service, protecting the Eastern borders of the Kingdom of Hungary in the Middle Ages.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-26) Before the Communist era and the collectivization efforts, the Csángós were structured in a traditional society until the introduction of [civil code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Code_of_Romania). Village elders were well respected and could be pointed out by their traditionally long hair and beards. Notably, some Csángós also participated in the [1907 Romanian Peasants' Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1907_Romanian_Peasants%27_Revolt) and fought on behalf of Romania in both world wars.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-27)

2001 Report of the Council of Europe

Coat of arms of the Csángós

For centuries, the self-identity of the Csángós was based on the Roman Catholic religion and the Hungarian language spoken in the family. It is generally accepted by serious scholars (Hungarian but also Romanian) that the Csángós have a [Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_people) origin, and that they arrived in Moldavia from the west. Some Romanian authors claim that the Csángós are in fact "Magyarised" Romanians from [Transylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transylvania). This theory has also to be dismissed; it is not conceivable that these "[Romanians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_people)" could persist in using a "foreign" language after centuries of living in Romania surrounded by Romanians speaking Romanian. Whatever can be argued about the language of the Csángós there is no doubt that this is a form of Hungarian.- Csango minority culture in Romania, Doc. 9078 from 4 May 2001[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Committee-7)

The Council of Europe has expressed its concerns about the situation of the Csángó minority culture,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Council-8) and discussed that the Csángós speak an early form of Hungarian and are associated with ancient traditions, and a great diversity of folk art and culture, which is of exceptional value for Europe. The council also mentioned that (although not everybody agrees on this number) it is thought that between 60,000 and 70,000 people speak the Csángó Hungarian dialect. It has also expressed concerns that despite the provisions of the Romanian law on education, and repeated requests from parents there is no teaching of the Csángó language in the Csángó villages, and, as a consequence, very few Csángós are able to write in their mother tongue. The document also discussed that the Csángós make no political demands, but merely want to be recognized as a distinct culture and demand education and church services in the Csángó dialect.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Committee-7)

At the time of this report's release, the [Vatican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_See) expressed hope that the Csángós would be able to celebrate Catholic masses in their [liturgical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liturgy) language, Csángó.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-30)

Comments of the government of Romania, dissenting opinion on behalf of the Romanian delegation

The situation of the Csángó community may be understood by taking into consideration the results of the [2002 census](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=2002_Romanian_census&action=edit&redlink=1). 1,370 persons declared themselves Csángó.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-census-31) Most of them live in [Bacău County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bac%C4%83u_County), Romania, and belong to the [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church). During the last years, some statements identified all [Catholics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholics) in Bacău County (119.618 persons according to 2002 census) as Csángó. This identification is rejected by most of them, who did identify themselves as Romanians.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Comments-32)

The name Csángó appeared relatively recently, being used for the first time, in 1780 by Péter Zöld.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Dissent-33) The name Csángó is used to describe two different ethnic groups:

those concentrated in the county of Bacău (the southern group) and in the area surrounding the city of Roman (the northern group). We know for certain that these people are not Szeklers. They are Romanian in appearance, and the majority of them speak a Transylvanian dialect of Romanian and live according to Romanian traditions and customs. These characteristics suggest that they are Romanians from Transylvania who have joined the Romanian Catholic population of Moldavia.

those of Szekler origin, most of whom settled in the valleys of the Trotuş and the Tazlău and, to a lesser extent, of the Siret. Their mother tongue is the same as that spoken by the Szeklers, and they live side by side with Romanians.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Dissent-33)

Hungarian sources

Their music shows the characteristic features of [Hungarian music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_folk_music) and the words of their songs are mostly Hungarian, with some dialect differences.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-JSTOR-34)

The anthem of the Csángós[[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify) (see [Talk:Csángós/Archive 2 § Anthem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s/Archive_2#Anthem))] refers to Csángó Hungarians multiple times.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3_Himnusz-35)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Horv%C3%A1th_1999-36)

The Csángós did not take part in the language reforms of the Age of Enlightenment, or the bourgeois transformation that created the modern consciousness of nationhood (cf. Halász 1992, Kósa 1998). They did not have a noble stratum or intelligentsia (cf. Kósa 1981) that could have fashioned their consciousness as Hungarians (Halász 1992: 11). They were "saved" (Kósa 1998: 339) from "assimilation" with the Romanians by virtue of their Roman Catholic religion, which distinguished them from the majority Greek Orthodox society.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-37)

Hungarian sources

In 2001 the Romanian authorities banned the teaching of the Hungarian language in private houses in the village of [Cleja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleja), despite the recommendation of the Council of Europe.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-39)[[need quotation to verify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability)] From 1990, parents in [Cleja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleja), [Pustiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pustiana) and [Lespezi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lespezi) requested several times that their children have the opportunity of learning the Hungarian language at school, either as an optional language, or as their native language, in 1-4 lessons a week. At best their petition was registered, but in most cases it was ignored. Seeing the possibility of organizing Hungarian courses outside school, they gave up the humiliating process of writing requests without results. The MCSMSZ maintains its standpoint according to which the community should claim their legal rights, but the population is not so determined. Leaders of the school inspectorate in Bacău County, as well as the authorities and church, declared at a meeting that they were opposed to the official instruction of Hungarian in Csángó villages. In their opinion the Csángós are of Romanian origin, and sporadic requests for teaching Hungarian at schools reflect not a real parental demand, but Hungarian nationalist ambitions.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-40)[[independent source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Independent_sources)]

In the village of Arini (Magyarfalu in Hungarian) the village mayor and the Romanian-only teachers of the state school, filed a complaint with the local police about the "unlawful teaching activities" of Gergely Csoma. Csoma teaches[[timeframe?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Relative_time_references)] Hungarian as an extracurricular activity to the children of Arini. Following the complaint, the local police started what Csángó activists have described as an intimidation campaign among the mothers of those children who are studying their maternal language with the said teacher.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-41)

In 2008 members of the [European Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Parliament) sent a petition to the [European Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Commission) regarding the obstruction of Hungarian language education and the alleged intimidation of Csángó-Hungarian pupils in Valea Mare (Nagypatak).[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-EC-42) The leader of the High Commission on Minority Affairs responded to the petition of [László Tőkés](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_T%C5%91k%C3%A9s) [MEP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament) in a written notice that they would warn Romania to secure education in the mother tongue for the Csángós of Moldavia.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-EC-42)

Romanian sources

According to the final report of the [Presidential Commission for the Study of the Communist Dictatorship in Romania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_Commission_for_the_Study_of_the_Communist_Dictatorship_in_Romania) in 2006, the policy of assimilation of the communist regime had serious consequences on the situation of the Csángós in Moldavia. The report noted that the first attempts at forced assimilation of the Csángós date back to the interwar period, with the Catholic Church taking on an important role in this process. Facilitating the loss of the linguistic identity of the Csángós allowed the Catholic Church to stop their assimilation into the Orthodox Church, and as a result of these policies, the Csángós did not benefit from religious services and education in their mother tongue.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-43)

Population

It is difficult to estimate the exact number of the Csángós because of the elusive nature and multiple factors (ethnicity, religion and language) of Csángó identity.[[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]

As far as ethnic identification is concerned, in the census of 2002, 4,317 declared themselves [Hungarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magyar_people) and 796 declared themselves Csángó in Bacău County, reaching a total of 5,794 out of the county's total population of 706,623. The report of the Council of Europe estimates a Csángó population ranging from 20,000 to as many as 260,000, based in the total Catholic population in the area, which may be an exaggeration as there also are Catholic ethnic Romanians.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Committee-7)[[dead link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot)] One plausible explanation for this discrepancy is that many Csángó hide or disguise their true ethnicity.[[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]

The Council of Europe had in 2001 estimates that put the total number of Csángó-speaking people between 60,000 and 70,000.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-Council-8)

According to the most recent research executed between 2008 and 2010 by Vilmos Tánczos, famous Hungarian folklorist, there has been a sharp decline in the total number of Csángó-speaking people in Eastern Romania. Tánczos set their number to roughly 43,000 people. Moreover, he found out that the most archaic version of Csángó language, the Northern Csángó was known and regularly used by only some 4,000 people, exclusively the older generation above the age of 50. It can be said, therefore, that the Csángó Hungarian dialect is at high risk of extinction. In fact, when applying the UNESCO Framework to measure language vitality, this dialect fits the category of "Severely Endangered".[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s#cite_note-44)

## Fuente en español: Dialecto csango

Dialecto csango

El dialecto csango (del [húngaro](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idioma_h%C3%BAngaro) csángó, pronúnciese "chaangoo" ['tʃa:ngo:]) es una variedad arcaica del húngaro, hablada actualmente por unas 13.000 personas en [Moldavia Occidental](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moldavia_Occidental) ([Bacău](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bac%C4%83u)), en [Rumania](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rumania). Es el único [dialecto](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialecto) histórico del húngaro con una evolución propia desde el húngaro medieval. El csango difiere del húngaro, sobre todo, en [pronunciación](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pronunciaci%C3%B3n) y [léxico](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9xico). Un rasgo propio de este dialecto es la /s/ [apicoalveolar](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fricativa_apicoalvelar_sorda?action=edit&redlink=1) (como en el [castellano](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idioma_espa%C3%B1ol) [peninsular](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialecto_castellano_septentrional)), y la presencia de la [prepalatal](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prepalatal) [africada](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africada) sonora dzs en lugar de la [palatal](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palatal) [oclusiva](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oclusiva) sonora gy del húngaro.

Para hablantes del húngaro el csango es muy difícil de entender, sobre todo porque conserva muchos arcaísmos léxicos del húngaro medieval. Las variedades húngaras más cercanas al csango son las hablas de las minorías húngaras (los [székely](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sz%C3%A9kely)) de [Transilvania](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transilvania). ## Source: Csango Land

Csango Land

Csango Land ([Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_language): Csangofold; [Romanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_language): Tara Ceangailor, Tinutul Ceangaiesc or Ceangaimea) is the name given to the region in [Western Moldavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Moldavia), in turn a region of [Romania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romania), where most of the [Csangos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3s), a small subgroup of the [Hungarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians), live. Csango Land is located close to the [Eastern Carpathian Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisions_of_the_Carpathians), in the valley of the [Siret River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siret_(river)), near the town of [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman,_Romania) and [Bacau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bac%C4%83u). It may also be defined as the part of [Bacau County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bac%C4%83u_County) where ethnic Hungarians reside as a minority.

The Csangos, although mostly living in [Moldavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moldavia), also live in [Transylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transylvania) (part of Romania as well), precisely in two zones. These are the area around the [Ghimes-Palanca Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghime%C8%99-Palanca_Pass) and the so-called [Seven Villages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Villages). Additionally, there is a Csango village in [Northern Dobruja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Dobruja), a region also in Romania, known as [Oituz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lumina,_Constan%C8%9Ba) (in [Constanta County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constan%C8%9Ba_County)).

The Csangos speak a [Hungarian dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_dialects) known as [Csango](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1ng%C3%B3_dialect). The [Council of Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Europe) claimed the number of speakers of this dialect to be of 60,000 to 70,000 people in 2001. However, in the [2011 Romanian census](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_Romanian_census), only 4,208 (0.68%) and 829 (0.13%) of the inhabitants of Bacau County declared themselves Hungarian and Csango, respectively.

Important distinction: the 0.68% and 0.13% figures refer to ethnic self-identification in Bacau County in the 2011 Romanian census. They are not the percentage of Csango people who speak the Csango dialect. The available text gives speaker counts, including an older 2001 estimate of 60,000 to 70,000 speakers from the Council of Europe and 13,000 speakers in the Spanish source on the Csango dialect, but it does not provide a direct percentage of Csango people who speak the dialect.

The Csangos live near a much larger subgroup of Hungarians, the [Szekelys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sz%C3%A9kelys), who also live in the eastern [Carpathian Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains), in Transylvania. They also have a region named after them, [Szekely Land](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sz%C3%A9kely_Land), divided mostly between the [Covasna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covasna_County), [Harghita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harghita_County), and [Mures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mure%C8%99_County) counties of Romania. The Szekelys have strived for making Szekely Land an autonomous region in Romania. This is not the case of the Csangos, who have not made any request for autonomy.

## Source: Csango flag

The flag used for the Csango ethnic group was adopted by the Csango Council.

The flag has four fields divided by a broad silver or light grey cross. The upper-left field is red and contains a golden sun. The upper-right field is black and contains a silver or light grey crescent moon. The lower-left field is black. The lower-right field is red.

Available information identifies the Csango Council as the body that adopted the flag, but it does not specify the individual designer or creator.
